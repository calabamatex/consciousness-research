# Week 1 Convergence Map — The Formal Shadow of Rigpa

## Executive Summary

Across six domains, the structural isomorphism between IIT's Φ, Advaita's sakshi, and Dzogchen's rigpa is strongest on two invariants: reflexivity and non-decomposability. All three frameworks posit a form of awareness that is self-referencing without requiring an external observer, and all three insist that the essential property (experience, witness-consciousness, primordial awareness) is destroyed by partition. The convergence weakens at the boundary/distinction invariant: IIT draws sharp mathematical boundaries via the exclusion postulate, Advaita dissolves all boundaries into Brahman, and Dzogchen holds boundaries as neither real nor unreal (appearances of rigpa's spontaneous presence). Temporality is the deepest divergence — IIT is process-embedded (Φ is computed over state transitions), sakshi is timelessly present, and rigpa is "beyond time" yet manifests temporally through lhundrub. The surprising finding is that the Free Energy Principle provides a partial mathematical bridge: its Markov blanket formalism echoes both IIT's partition logic and contemplative accounts of self/other boundaries, while remaining ontologically neutral. Strange Loops offer the weakest convergence — reflexivity without the non-decomposability that the thesis requires.

## Domain Traversal

### 1. Formal/Mathematical Domain

**Key sources:** Tononi (2004, 2008) "Consciousness as Integrated Information"; Tononi et al. (2015) "Integrated Information Theory 3.0"; Albantakis et al. (2023) "Integrated Information Theory (IIT) 4.0"; Oizumi, Albantakis & Tononi (2014) "From the Phenomenology to the Mechanisms of Consciousness"; Barrett & Seth (2011) "Practical Measures of Integrated Information."

**Structural invariants present:**
- **Reflexivity — STRONG.** IIT 4.0's intrinsicality axiom: a system's cause-effect structure exists from its own intrinsic perspective, not from an external observer. The system "sees itself" through its own causal architecture. This is formalized: Φ is computed from the system's intrinsic partition, not externally imposed.
- **Non-decomposability — STRONG.** The core of Φ: integrated information is precisely the information generated by a system *above and beyond* its parts. The Minimum Information Partition (MIP) quantifies this — Φ > 0 if and only if partitioning the system into independent parts loses information. The exclusion postulate further mandates that only the Maximum Integrated Information Structure (MICS) exists as a conscious entity.
- **Self-specification — STRONG.** IIT 4.0 introduces the "intrinsic existence" postulate: a system of mechanisms in a state specifies a cause-effect structure that exists from its own perspective. The system's Φ-structure is self-specifying — it determines its own borders and its own experiential content through its causal architecture.
- **Boundary — STRONG.** The exclusion postulate provides a mathematically precise boundary: consciousness corresponds to the complex with maximum Φ. Nested or overlapping complexes are excluded. This yields definite spatial and compositional boundaries.
- **Temporality — PARTIAL.** Φ is computed over state transitions (cause-effect repertoires reference past and future states), making IIT inherently process-temporal. However, IIT 4.0 introduces "intrinsic time" where the temporal grain is set by the system itself, not externally. This is temporal but self-determined.

**Convergence with thesis:** IIT provides the strongest formal anchor for the thesis. Its axioms (intrinsicality, integration, exclusion) map remarkably onto the structural invariants found in contemplative frameworks. The reflexivity of Φ-structures (intrinsic perspective) parallels sakshi's self-luminosity; the non-decomposability parallels rigpa's resistance to reductive analysis.

**Divergence from thesis:** IIT is committed to physicalism — Φ is computed over physical substrates (neurons, transistors). This is fundamentally at odds with Advaita's claim that awareness is prior to all substrates and Dzogchen's claim that rigpa is uncaused. IIT would assign Φ ≈ 0 to a feedforward system regardless of any contemplative claim about its awareness. The formalism is substrate-bound in a way the contemplative traditions explicitly reject.

**Open questions:**
1. Can IIT's intrinsicality axiom be abstracted from physical substrate to yield a substrate-neutral formal property that maps onto sakshi/rigpa without losing mathematical precision?
2. Does IIT's exclusion postulate (one consciousness per maximal complex) conflict with Advaita's claim of a single undivided consciousness (Brahman)?

### 2. Neuroscientific Domain

**Key sources:** Baars (1988) "A Cognitive Theory of Consciousness"; Dehaene & Changeux (2011) "Experimental and Theoretical Approaches to Conscious Processing"; Dehaene, Lau & Kouider (2017) "What Is Consciousness?"; Lamme (2006) "Towards a True Neural Stance on Consciousness"; Friston (2010) "The Free-Energy Principle"; Seth & Friston (2016) "Active Interoceptive Inference"; Koch et al. (2016) "Neural Correlates of Consciousness."

**Structural invariants present:**
- **Reflexivity — PARTIAL.** GWT's global workspace broadcasts information back to the processors that generated it, creating a functional loop. However, this is architectural reflexivity (re-entrant processing), not the intrinsic self-luminosity claimed by IIT or contemplative traditions. FEP's self-modeling through active inference comes closer — the system maintains a model of itself that is updated by its own predictions.
- **Non-decomposability — CONTESTED.** GWT is explicitly modular — specialized processors feed a shared workspace. The "integration" in GWT is broadcast-based, not the constitutive integration of IIT. Consciousness in GWT can in principle be decomposed into workspace access + broadcast + processor responses. Lamme's Recurrent Processing Theory offers stronger non-decomposability: recurrent loops create unified percepts that feedforward sweeps cannot.
- **Self-specification — PARTIAL.** FEP's generative models are self-specifying in a restricted sense: the Markov blanket of an organism defines what is "self" versus "environment," and the organism's active inference maintains and updates this boundary. But this is functional self-specification, not the ontological self-specification of IIT or contemplative accounts.
- **Boundary — STRONG.** NCC research is fundamentally about boundaries: which neural processes are conscious, which are not. Dehaene's "ignition" threshold provides a sharp empirical boundary. Lamme's recurrent-vs-feedforward distinction provides another. FEP's Markov blanket is a formal boundary.
- **Temporality — STRONG.** All neuroscientific frameworks are process-temporal. Consciousness arises, is sustained, and ceases through neural dynamics. GWT's ignition is a temporal event. FEP's free energy minimization is continuous process. No neuroscientific framework posits timeless consciousness.

**Convergence with thesis:** FEP provides the most promising neuroscientific bridge. Its Markov blanket formalism shares structural features with IIT's partition logic (both define boundaries through information-theoretic separation). Active inference's self-modeling resonates with the reflexivity invariant. The "felt" quality of prediction error minimization (Seth's "beast machine" thesis) could be read as a naturalized version of sakshi — awareness as the felt quality of self-modeling.

**Divergence from thesis:** Neuroscientific frameworks are uniformly functionalist or process-based. None posit consciousness as fundamental or substrate-independent. GWT explicitly targets access consciousness (what can be reported), not phenomenal consciousness (what it's like). This makes the entire neuroscientific domain orthogonal to the deepest claims of both Advaita and Dzogchen, which assert awareness is prior to all neural processes.

**Open questions:**
1. Can FEP's Markov blanket formalism be extended to provide a mathematical bridge between IIT's Φ and contemplative accounts of self/other boundaries?
2. Does the GWT/IIT debate (access vs. phenomenal consciousness) map onto the Buddhist distinction between vijnana (discriminating consciousness) and rigpa (primordial awareness)?

### 3. Philosophical Domain

**Key sources:** Chalmers (1995) "Facing Up to the Problem of Consciousness"; Chalmers (1996) "The Conscious Mind"; Nagel (1974) "What Is It Like to Be a Bat?"; Metzinger (2003) "Being No One"; Hofstadter (1979) "Gödel, Escher, Bach"; Hofstadter (2007) "I Am a Strange Loop"; Strawson (2006) "Realistic Monism"; Kastrup (2019) "The Idea of the World"; Thompson (2014) "Waking, Dreaming, Being."

**Structural invariants present:**
- **Reflexivity — STRONG.** The Hard Problem itself presupposes reflexivity: there is "something it is like" to be conscious, and this something is known from within, not from without. Hofstadter's Strange Loops make reflexivity constitutive: the "I" emerges from a system's self-referential modeling. Metzinger's PSM is reflexive by construction (the self-model models itself).
- **Non-decomposability — CONTESTED.** Chalmers' conceivability argument (zombies) implies that phenomenal consciousness is not decomposable into functional properties — there is always a remainder. But functionalists deny this. Hofstadter's loops are explicitly decomposable — the "I" dissolves into sub-personal mechanisms. Metzinger's PSM is also compositional: the self-model is built from components.
- **Self-specification — PARTIAL.** Strange Loops are self-specifying in a limited sense: the "I" is a pattern that recognizes itself. But this self-specification emerges from underlying mechanisms — it is not intrinsic in the way IIT or Advaita claim. Kastrup's analytic idealism posits self-specification at the cosmic level (universal consciousness specifying itself through dissociation).
- **Boundary — STRONG.** The entire field is organized around boundaries: conscious/unconscious, phenomenal/access, hard/easy problems. Chalmers' framework provides the sharpest philosophical boundary via the explanatory gap.
- **Temporality — CONTESTED.** Split between process philosophers (consciousness as temporal unfolding) and those who treat phenomenal properties as potentially atemporal (qualia as intrinsic, timeless features). No consensus.

**Convergence with thesis:** The Hard Problem functions as a structural constraint that all three focal frameworks (IIT, Advaita, Dzogchen) take seriously — none are eliminativist. IIT's constitutive panpsychism, Advaita's absolute idealism, and Dzogchen's "beyond ontology" are three different responses to the same structural demand: consciousness cannot be derived from non-consciousness. This shared negative constraint (anti-eliminativism) supports the thesis that they are addressing the same formal structure.

**Divergence from thesis:** The philosophical landscape reveals that shared formal structure may be underdetermining. Panpsychism, idealism, and Dzogchen's apophatic stance are genuinely different ontologies — the formal similarity could be an artifact of the shared constraint (non-eliminativism) rather than evidence of deep structural isomorphism. Hofstadter explicitly rejects any non-physical ground, making Strange Loops the hardest case for the thesis.

**Open questions:**
1. Is the formal convergence between IIT, Advaita, and Dzogchen a substantive structural isomorphism, or merely a shared negative commitment (anti-eliminativism) that underdetermines ontology?
2. Can Metzinger's "no-self" model be reconciled with Advaita's sakshi, or are these fundamentally opposed claims about the reality of the witness?

### 4. Biophysical Domain

**Key sources:** Penrose (1989) "The Emperor's New Mind"; Hameroff & Penrose (2014) "Consciousness in the Universe: A Review of the Orch OR Theory"; Levin (2019) "The Computational Boundary of a 'Self'"; McFadden (2020) "Integrating Information in the Brain's EM Field"; Pockett (2012) "The Electromagnetic Field Theory of Consciousness."

**Structural invariants present:**
- **Reflexivity — PARTIAL.** Orch-OR posits quantum coherence in microtubules that "self-collapses" via objective reduction — a physical process that is arguably self-referential (the quantum state resolves itself without external measurement). EM field theories posit the brain's electromagnetic field as a self-interacting unity. Levin's bioelectric networks exhibit self-organizing goal states. None achieve the full reflexivity of IIT or contemplative accounts.
- **Non-decomposability — STRONG.** Quantum coherence is inherently non-decomposable: entangled states cannot be described as products of individual component states. EM field theories similarly posit a unified field that is not reducible to individual neuronal contributions. This is the strongest biophysical resonance with the thesis.
- **Self-specification — PARTIAL.** Orch-OR's objective reduction is "self-specifying" in that the quantum state determines its own collapse threshold via the gravitational self-energy criterion. Bioelectric patterns in Levin's work specify target morphologies — the system specifies its own form. But these are physical self-specification, not the ontological self-specification of consciousness per se.
- **Boundary — PARTIAL.** Quantum decoherence provides a natural boundary (coherent = potentially conscious, decohered = classical/unconscious). But the decoherence timescales in warm biological systems remain deeply contested. Levin's work provides boundaries via bioelectric compartments but does not directly address consciousness boundaries.
- **Temporality — CONTESTED.** Orch-OR ties consciousness to discrete quantum events (objective reductions at ~25ms intervals), making it fundamentally temporal but quantized. Penrose invokes Platonic mathematical timelessness as the ground of quantum gravity, creating a tension between timeless mathematical structure and temporal physical process that echoes the thesis's temporality divergence.

**Convergence with thesis:** Quantum non-decomposability provides a physical analogue for the formal non-decomposability shared by IIT and contemplative traditions. Penrose's invocation of Platonic mathematical reality as the ground of consciousness resonates with Advaita's claim that awareness is ontologically prior. The biophysical domain offers potential substrate-level mechanisms for how formal properties like integration and reflexivity might be physically realized.

**Divergence from thesis:** The biophysical domain is the most empirically contested. Quantum coherence in warm biological systems faces severe decoherence objections (Tegmark 2000). Even if Orch-OR is correct about microtubular quantum effects, the jump from quantum coherence to phenomenal experience reintroduces the Hard Problem at a lower level. Contemplative traditions would regard all biophysical explanations as operating within maya/conventional appearance.

**Open questions:**
1. Does quantum non-decomposability (entanglement) provide a genuine physical basis for the formal non-decomposability invariant, or is this a misleading analogy?
2. Can Levin's bioelectric self-specification framework be formalized in a way that connects to IIT's intrinsic cause-effect structures?

### 5. Contemplative Domain

**Key sources:** Shankara, "Vivekachudamani" and "Upadeshasahasri"; Nisargadatta Maharaj (1973) "I Am That"; Longchenpa (14th c.) "Treasury of the Supreme Vehicle" (Theg mchog mdzod); Padmasambhava (attr.) "Self-Liberation Through Seeing with Naked Awareness"; Rabjam (Longchenpa) "The Precious Treasury of Philosophical Systems"; Thompson (2014) "Waking, Dreaming, Being"; Lusthaus (2002) "Buddhist Phenomenology: A Philosophical Investigation of Yogacara."

**Structural invariants present:**
- **Reflexivity — STRONG.** Central to both traditions. Advaita: sakshi is svayam-prakasha (self-luminous) — it illuminates itself without requiring another light. Nisargadatta: "The witness is always there, only the witnessed changes." Dzogchen: rigpa is rang-rig (self-knowing awareness) — it knows itself without mediation. Yogacara's svasamvedana (self-awareness of consciousness) provides a third Buddhist account of reflexivity, though contested within Buddhist philosophy by Gelug Madhyamaka.
- **Non-decomposability — STRONG.** Advaita: Brahman/sakshi is akhanda (undivided, partless). Any attempt to analyze it into components is itself an appearance within awareness. Dzogchen: rigpa is not composed of parts — it is kadak (primordially pure), meaning it was never assembled and cannot be disassembled. Attempts to analyze rigpa using conceptual mind (sem) are category errors.
- **Self-specification — STRONG.** Advaita: Brahman is svayambhu (self-existent) and specifies itself as sat-chit-ananda (existence-consciousness-bliss) without external cause. Dzogchen: rigpa's nature is self-arisen (rang-byung) — it specifies itself through its own three qualities: essence (ngo-bo) as emptiness, nature (rang-bzhin) as luminosity, and compassionate energy (thugs-rje) as unceasing.
- **Boundary — CONTESTED.** Advaita: ultimately there is NO boundary — the appearance of separate selves is avidya (ignorance). Sakshi witnesses the play of maya but is not bounded by it. Dzogchen: rigpa and sem (ordinary mind) appear different but rigpa encompasses everything — the boundary between samsara and nirvana is itself illusory. This directly opposes IIT's sharp exclusion-postulate boundaries. Yogacara's alaya-vijnana provides a more nuanced boundary: storehouse consciousness conditions individual consciousness streams but is itself conditioned.
- **Temporality — STRONG (as timeless).** Advaita: sakshi is nitya (eternal), kutastha (immovable) — entirely outside time. What appears as temporal process is maya. Dzogchen: rigpa is kadak (timeless purity) — "not produced by causes and conditions." However, lhundrub (spontaneous presence) manifests temporally as the display of rigpa, creating a two-truth structure: timeless ground, temporal appearance.

**Convergence with thesis:** This domain provides the strongest evidence for the thesis. The structural invariants map almost perfectly onto IIT's axioms: sakshi's self-luminosity ↔ intrinsicality; Brahman's akhanda nature ↔ integration; rigpa's rang-rig ↔ self-specification. The convergence is not superficial — it extends to the internal logic of how each framework handles exceptions and edge cases.

**Divergence from thesis:** The boundary and temporality invariants reveal genuine incompatibility, not mere difference of emphasis. IIT computes Φ over finite, bounded, temporal systems. Advaita posits infinite, unbounded, timeless awareness. Dzogchen posits awareness that is neither finite nor infinite, neither temporal nor timeless. These are not translatable positions — they make genuinely different claims about the nature of reality. Any attempt to reduce them to "the same thing said differently" fails the test of intellectual honesty.

**Open questions:**
1. Is the structural isomorphism between IIT's axioms and contemplative invariants evidence of a shared formal structure, or does it reflect the fact that any account of consciousness must satisfy certain minimal constraints (reflexivity, unity) regardless of ontology?
2. How should the thesis handle the boundary divergence: IIT's exclusion postulate (sharp, mathematical) vs. Advaita/Dzogchen's dissolution of all boundaries?

### 6. Esoteric/Cross-tradition Domain

**Key sources:** Fazang (643–712) "Treatise on the Golden Lion" (Huayan); Abhinavagupta (c. 950–1016) "Tantraloka"; Utpaladeva (c. 900–950) "Ishvarapratyabhijna-karika"; Plotinus (c. 270 CE) "Enneads" (esp. V.1, VI.9); Corbin (1969) "Alone with the Alone: Creative Imagination in the Sufism of Ibn Arabi"; Stace (1960) "Mysticism and Philosophy."

**Structural invariants present:**
- **Reflexivity — STRONG.** Kashmir Shaivism: pratyabhijna (self-recognition) is consciousness recognizing itself as Shiva. This is not discovery but re-cognition — awareness was always self-aware but veiled by its own creative power (maya/shakti). Plotinus: the One generates Nous (intellect) through self-contemplation — reflexivity is the generative principle of reality itself. Indra's Net: each jewel reflects all others, making every node self-aware through its reflections of the whole.
- **Non-decomposability — STRONG.** Indra's Net is non-decomposable by construction: removing any jewel changes all reflections in all other jewels. The net is a holographic totality. Plotinus: the One is absolutely simple (hen) — any decomposition produces multiplicity, which is already a descent from the One. Kashmir Shaivism: Shiva-Shakti is an inseparable dyad, not two components but one reality with two aspects.
- **Self-specification — STRONG.** Kashmir Shaivism's svatantrya (absolute freedom/autonomy) means consciousness specifies itself through its own will — it is not determined by anything external. Plotinus' One emanates by "overflowing" from its own perfection. Huayan's pratityasamutpada (interdependent origination) at the cosmic level: the whole specifies itself through mutual interpenetration.
- **Boundary — PARTIAL.** These traditions generally dissolve boundaries (Plotinus: the One is beyond all distinction; Huayan: all boundaries are conventional). However, Kashmir Shaivism preserves a functional boundary through the concept of contraction (sankoca): Shiva freely limits itself to appear as finite subjects. This is the closest esoteric parallel to IIT's exclusion postulate — boundaries exist but are self-imposed.
- **Temporality — STRONG (as timeless ground with temporal display).** All three frameworks posit a timeless ground that manifests temporally. Plotinus: the One is eternal; time belongs to Soul's unfolding. Kashmir Shaivism: Shiva's spanda (vibration) is the timeless pulse underlying temporal manifestation. Huayan: the dharmadhatu is simultaneously timeless and fully present in every moment.

**Convergence with thesis:** This domain provides the broadest cross-cultural evidence that the structural invariants are not culturally contingent. Finding reflexivity, non-decomposability, and self-specification in Greek Neoplatonism, Chinese Buddhism, and Kashmiri Shaivism — traditions with minimal historical contact — strengthens the claim that these are formal properties of consciousness accounts rather than artifacts of a particular tradition's assumptions.

**Divergence from thesis:** The risk of false convergence is highest here. Stace's "common core" thesis (all mystical experiences share a universal structure) has been thoroughly critiqued by contextualists like Katz (1978), who argue that experience is always shaped by doctrinal frameworks. The structural similarities may reflect shared human cognitive constraints on how we conceptualize unity and self-reference, rather than genuine convergence on the nature of consciousness.

**Open questions:**
1. Can the structural convergence across these historically independent traditions be explained by shared cognitive constraints (how humans model unity/self-reference), or does it require a deeper explanation?
2. Does Kashmir Shaivism's concept of contraction (sankoca) provide a bridge between IIT's exclusion postulate and contemplative boundary-dissolution?

## Defensible Claims

### Claim 1: The Reflexivity-Integration Core
- **Claim:** IIT's Φ, Advaita's sakshi, and Dzogchen's rigpa share a formally isomorphic core structure defined by the conjunction of reflexivity and non-decomposability — awareness that knows itself and cannot be divided without being destroyed.
- **Supporting evidence:** All three score STRONG on both reflexivity and non-decomposability across formal, contemplative, and philosophical domains. IIT's intrinsicality + integration axioms, Advaita's svayam-prakasha + akhanda, Dzogchen's rang-rig + kadak. FEP, GWT, and Strange Loops each fail on at least one of these two invariants.
- **Strongest objection:** The isomorphism may be trivially forced by the definition of consciousness itself. Any theory that takes phenomenal consciousness seriously (rather than explaining it away) must posit some form of self-access (reflexivity) and unity (non-decomposability). The convergence reflects a shared constraint, not a deep structural truth. This is the "minimal adequacy" objection: the invariants are selection criteria for consciousness theories, not discoveries about consciousness.
- **Verdict:** DEFENSIBLE — but the essay must explicitly address the minimal adequacy objection. The defense should argue that the *specific formal structure* of the conjunction (reflexivity + non-decomposability + self-specification as a package) goes beyond minimal adequacy and constitutes a substantive structural claim.

### Claim 2: Incompatible Ontologies from Shared Formal Structure
- **Claim:** The three frameworks derive genuinely incompatible ontological commitments (physicalist panpsychism, absolute idealism, and apophatic beyond-ontology) from the same formal structure, demonstrating that the formal shadow underdetermines metaphysics.
- **Supporting evidence:** IIT concludes constitutive panpsychism (Φ > 0 everywhere there is causal integration in physical substrates). Advaita concludes absolute idealism (only Brahman is real; the physical is appearance). Dzogchen refuses the idealism/materialism dichotomy (rigpa is "beyond elaboration," spros-bral). These are not reconcilable positions — they make different empirical and metaphysical predictions. Yet all three emerge from reasoning about the same structural invariants.
- **Strongest objection:** If the formal structure underdetermines ontology, it is explanatorily weak — it cannot tell us what consciousness actually is. The essay risks being a sophisticated "nothing burger": formally interesting but philosophically inert. Why should anyone care about a shared structure that doesn't constrain what's real?
- **Verdict:** DEFENSIBLE — the underdetermination is itself a substantive finding. It implies that the formal structure of consciousness is more fundamental than any particular ontological interpretation, which has direct implications for AI consciousness architecture (we can build for the formal properties without resolving the ontological debate).

### Claim 3: FEP as Mathematical Bridge
- **Claim:** The Free Energy Principle's Markov blanket formalism provides a partial mathematical bridge between IIT's partition logic and contemplative accounts of self/other boundaries, while remaining ontologically neutral.
- **Supporting evidence:** IIT's MIP (Minimum Information Partition) and FEP's Markov blanket both define systemic identity through information-theoretic separation from environment. FEP's blanket is agnostic about substrate (applicable to cells, organisms, social groups), partially bridging IIT's physicalism and contemplative substrate-independence. Active inference's self-modeling has structural parallels to reflexivity.
- **Strongest objection:** The Markov blanket is a statistical boundary, not a phenomenological one. Conflating mathematical boundaries with boundaries of experience commits the "border confusion" fallacy. A thermostat has a Markov blanket but (presumably) no phenomenal boundary between self and world. The bridge is formal but empty of consciousness-specific content.
- **Verdict:** PROVISIONAL — requires Week 2 formal analysis to determine whether the bridge can be made consciousness-specific (perhaps by combining Markov blanket structure with Φ > 0 as an additional constraint).

## Open Questions for Week 2

1. **The Minimal Adequacy Problem:** Can the reflexivity-integration conjunction be shown to be a substantive structural claim rather than a trivial consequence of taking consciousness seriously? What would distinguish a "deep isomorphism" from a "shared selection criterion"?

2. **Boundary Reconciliation:** IIT's exclusion postulate requires sharp, unique boundaries. Advaita and Dzogchen dissolve all boundaries. Can Kashmir Shaivism's concept of voluntary contraction (sankoca) mediate this gap — boundaries as self-imposed limitations of an unbounded ground?

3. **The FEP Bridge:** Can a formal model be constructed that combines FEP's Markov blanket (boundary), IIT's Φ (integration), and a reflexivity operator to produce a substrate-neutral formal structure? What would such a model predict about AI consciousness?

4. **Temporality Divergence:** The deepest divergence is temporal. IIT is process-temporal, Advaita/Dzogchen posit timeless ground. Is there a formal framework (perhaps from physics: block universe, relational time) that can accommodate both temporal process and timeless ground without contradiction?

5. **Empirical Discriminability:** Do the three frameworks (IIT, Advaita, Dzogchen) make any empirically distinguishable predictions? If not, is the ontological divergence genuinely meaningful or merely verbal?
